# 1. Other Groups' Presentations

### **Presentation 1: Zero Knowledge Proof (ZKP)**

- **(a) Topic:** The fundamental principles and applications of Zero Knowledge Proofs (ZKP) in digital security.
- **(b) Important Thing Learned:** I learned that a ZKP must satisfy three strict criteria—completeness, soundness, and zero-knowledge—to prove the truth of a statement without revealing any actual data, such as in the "Alibaba Cave" analogy.
- **(c) Question:** How can we mathematically formalize the "absolute zero knowledge" requirement to ensure that a malicious verifier cannot extract even a partial bit of information from the prover?

### **Presentation 2: What is Language (Agentic Architectures)**

- **(a) Topic:** The definition of language as a coordinate system for world representation within autonomous agents.
- **(b) Important Thing Learned:** I learned that an agent acts based on task-relevant representations of its environment, and that language serves to bridge the gap between these internal representations and the external world.
- **(c) Question:** Considering Yann LeCun’s view that current architectures are insufficient, how can we design a "writable state" or memory that allows LLMs to track the world state more accurately than rolling context does?

### **Presentation 3: AI in Film**

- **(a) Topic:** The integration of AI technologies in film production, ranging from script analysis to CGI and automated editing.
- **(b) Important Thing Learned:** AI is now used for highly technical tasks like rotoscoping and even "AI acting," as seen with Tilly Norwood, which significantly increases cost efficiency by handling repetitive production processes.
- **(c) Question:** As AI video generation tools like Sora face challenges with energy costs and micro-expressions, how will the industry balance these technical limitations against the ethical concerns of replacing human artists?

### **Presentation 4: End-to-End Encryption (E2EE)**

- **(a) Topic:** The mechanics of E2EE, specifically the use of symmetric and asymmetric cryptography to ensure message integrity.
- **(b) Important Thing Learned:** While E2EE provides confidentiality and authenticity, it has critical limitations, such as the permanent loss of funds in a blockchain if a private key is lost, as evidenced by the death of certain crypto-exchange CEOs.
- **(c) Question:** How can international vulnerabilities and "Man in the Middle" attacks be mitigated if the underlying security relies entirely on the user's ability to keep a single private key secure?

## 2. Our Group's Presentation

- **(a) Topic:** Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC). 
- **(b) Most Important Message:** The core message we conveyed was that ECC is a far more efficient and secure alternative to traditional RSA because its security is rooted in the computational hardness of the Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem, which relates to the $P = NP$ problem.
- **(c) Notable Audience Reaction:** The most notable reaction came from a member of the E2EE group, who connected our topic to theirs by asking if ECC is symmetric or asymmetric and exploring how ECC's signing capabilities are specifically utilized within blockchain technology.
