# 1. 构造观点的方法

**分点构造**
五个角度构造观点：
- 个人层面
- 社会层面
- 效率层面：是否提高了产出？
- 长期层面：短期的帮助怎么样？长期的帮助怎么样？
- 让步层面：是否在某些情况有害

比如题目：
“学生是否应该花更多时间学习历史？”
你可以迅速生成：
- 个人：学习历史能提升判断力，因为人能从过去的错误中吸取经验。
- 社会：历史教育能增强公民意识，让人理解制度、战争和社会冲突的来源。
- 长期：短期看历史不如技能课实用，但长期能帮助人形成更成熟的世界观。
- 让步：如果只是背日期和人物，历史课会低效；关键是要联系现实问题。

**按对象构造**
很多题目想不出点子，是因为只盯着抽象观点。你可以立刻问：“这个事情影响了哪些人？”

常见主体：
- 个人层面：学生，家长，老师，员工
- 社会层面：公司，政府，市场，消费者，社区，弱势群体，未来一代

例如题目：“公司是否应该允许员工在家办公？”
按主体拆：
- 员工：节省通勤时间，工作生活更平衡。
- 公司：减少办公室成本，但管理和沟通变难。
- 家庭：父母能更灵活照顾孩子。
- 社会：减少交通拥堵和污染。
- 新员工：可能难以融入团队、学习公司文化。

GRE中，加上带有条件的让步最好。一般情况下，我支持，但是在某些特殊情况下，我不支持。文章划分成同意和让步两部分。

“政府是否应该资助艺术？”
快速构造：
- 艺术从业者：鼓励艺术创造（融入后两点）
- 市场：艺术有公共价值，构成一种艺术的经济模式。
- 社会：艺术能保存文化身份，提升社会凝聚力。
- 让步：如果过于抽象，市场未必能充分支持。有的行为艺术甚至污染环境，堵塞交通。不支持。
- 让步：政府资助不应挤占医疗、教育等基本预算。
- 条件：资助应透明，并优先支持有公共教育价值的项目。


**常见观点：**

你可以记一些高频主题的“常用理由”。

**教育类**

- 培养批判性思维，而不是只记知识。
- 提高就业竞争力。
- 帮助学生发现兴趣。
- 促进公平，给低收入学生更多机会。
- 过度压力可能损害心理健康和创造力。

**科技类**

- 提高效率，降低成本。
- 扩大信息获取机会。
- 改变沟通和学习方式。
- 可能导致隐私问题、依赖、注意力下降。
- 技术本身中立，关键在使用方式和监管。

**政府类**

- 政府能提供公共产品，如教育、交通、医疗、环保。
- 政府需要保护弱势群体。
- 过度干预可能降低效率或限制自由。
- 政策应考虑成本、透明度和长期影响。

**工作类**

- 高薪能提高生活质量，但不一定带来满足感。
- 灵活工作能提高幸福感和效率。
- 团队合作促进创新。
- 竞争能激励进步，但过度竞争会造成压力。
- 工作意义、成长空间和人际关系也很重要。

**社会 / 文化类**

- 传统能提供身份认同和社会稳定。social cohesion
- 创新推动社会进步。
- 多元文化能扩大视野。
- 文化保护和经济发展可能冲突。
- 社会规范有时需要随着时代改变。

# 2. 每个观点段内逻辑的构造

想出一个点后，不要停在一句话。用这个链条扩展：观点 → 原因 → 结果 → 例子 → 对比

例如某段要写“大学生应该参加实习。”
- 观点：实习能帮助学生做职业选择。
- 原因：课堂知识通常比较抽象，学生不知道真实工作是什么样。
- 结果：通过实习，学生可以发现自己是否适合某个行业。
- 例子：一个学金融的学生在投行实习后，可能发现自己更喜欢数据分析而不是客户沟通。
- 让步：如果没有实习，他可能毕业后才发现职业不匹配，浪费更多时间。

例子的构造：我们可以想象一些非常general的例子而不局限于非常具体的例子。例如，要论证既成知识体系的过于成熟会阻碍新知，我原先举的例子是凝聚态的aderson开启新范式。但是完全可以举从牛顿力学到现代物理的过渡，或者哥白尼宇宙观的诞生。举的例子要有科普感。

把一个段落写长：我们遵循上述的基本扩展法则，每一个逻辑板块内的句子可以写相似意思的很多句，去翻来覆去地说。

# 3. 实践方法

看到题目后这样做：
1. **改写题目**：它到底在问什么选择？
2. **站队**：先选一个更容易写的立场，不一定是你真实想法。
3. **列主体**：谁受影响？
4. **列利弊**：至少写 2 个支持点 + 1 个反方点。
5. **加条件**：什么时候成立？什么时候不成立？
6. **选最容易举例的 2–3 个点写正文。**

# 6. 范文

**题目：**

Claim: An attitude of certainty undermines the advancement of knowledge of a subject.
Reason: The more certainty there is about a topic, the weaker the motivation to learn anything new about it.
Write a response in which you discuss the extent to which you agree or disagree with the claim and the reason on which that claim is based.

**范文：**

The advancement of human knowledge has always been driven by curiosity about the unknown, but when people develop an unshakable attitude of certainty toward a certain field, progress often grinds to a halt. I generally agree with the claim that an attitude of certainty undermines knowledge advancement, and I also concur with the underlying reason that excessive certainty erodes people’s motivation to pursue new discoveries. While basic consensus on established conclusions can lay a foundation for further research, rigid, absolute certainty almost always blocks intellectual progress.

In the field of social sciences and humanities, absolute certainty about traditional norms has long suppressed the emergence of new knowledge, exactly because it eliminates the motivation to question and explore. For centuries, societies across the world held an unchallenged, certain belief that inherent biological differences between men and women assign women to domestic roles and men to public careers. This certainty was so widely accepted that few scholars even thought to investigate whether this division of labor was socially constructed rather than naturally determined. For generations, no new meaningful knowledge about gender equality was produced because the dominant attitude of certainty killed any motivation to ask new questions or challenge existing norms. It was only when feminists challenged this absolute certainty and called the traditional view into question that the field of gender studies emerged, opening a brand new area of knowledge that transformed global understanding of human identity. Once the barrier of rigid certainty was removed, new discoveries were able to flourish.

The same pattern can also be observed in natural sciences, where absolute certainty about existing theories has repeatedly stalled progress. By the end of the 19th century, most physicists believed that the framework of Newtonian classical mechanics had already solved all fundamental problems in physics. Leading physicists openly claimed that there was nothing new to discover in physics, and all that remained was minor adjustments to measurement precision. This overwhelming attitude of certainty eliminated any motivation for young physicists to explore fundamental new questions about the universe. As the reason predicts, this near-complete certainty almost blocked the birth of modern physics: it took decades for revolutionary ideas like Einstein’s relativity and Planck’s quantum mechanics to gain widespread acceptance, because the dominant certainty of classical mechanics made most scholars unwilling to even consider these counterintuitive new theories. If the physics community had not held such a rigid attitude of certainty, the development of modern physics might have happened much earlier.

Admittedly, not all forms of certainty undermine knowledge advancement. Reasonable, provisional certainty about well-supported foundational theories actually provides a stable base for further exploration, rather than weakening motivation for new discoveries. For example, the core framework of evolutionary theory is now widely accepted as a certain conclusion by the biological community. This certainty does not stop biologists from exploring new frontiers of evolutionary research; instead, it gives them a shared foundation to investigate new questions such as the role of epigenetics in evolution, the mechanism of gene drift in small populations, and the evolutionary origin of human cognition. In this case, certainty is not an unchangeable dogma that closes off inquiry, but a working consensus that opens up new areas of research. The key difference here is that this certainty is provisional and open to revision, unlike the rigid attitude of absolute certainty that the original claim describes.

Overall, the original claim and its underlying reason are largely correct. A rigid attitude of absolute certainty toward any subject does erode the motivation to explore new knowledge, and thus undermines the advancement of the field. The exception of provisional foundational certainty does not refute the general rule, because it differs in nature from the inflexible certainty that closes off inquiry. For any field to keep progressing, scholars and the public need to avoid falling into the trap of absolute certainty, and maintain an open attitude toward new questions and new ideas.

**题目：**

Claim: Though often considered an objective pursuit, learning about the historical past requires creativity.  
  
Reason: Because we can never know the past directly, we must reconstruct it by imaginatively interpreting historical accounts, documents, and artifacts.  
  
Write a response in which you discuss the extent to which you agree or disagree with the claim and the reason on which the claim is based.

**范文：**

Many people regard the study of history as an inherently objective pursuit, where the only task of scholars is to dig out authentic documents and piece together an unalterable version of what happened in the past. However, as the claim argues, learning about the historical past actually requires considerable creativity, and this claim is rooted in the correct observation that we can never access the past directly and must reconstruct it through imaginative interpretation of historical remains. I fully agree with both the claim and its underlying reason: while history aims for objectivity, creativity is an indispensable core of historical research.

To begin with, the reason given by the claim accurately captures the fundamental nature of historical study. No modern historian or learner can travel back in time to experience the past directly; all we have left are fragmented artifacts, incomplete documents, and partial accounts passed down through generations. To turn these scattered remains into a coherent, meaningful historical narrative, creativity is absolutely necessary. A typical example is the interpretation of the Sanxingdui cultural relics discovered in southwest China. Dating back more than 3000 years, the site has yielded numerous exquisite bronze artifacts such as the 4-meter tall bronze sacred tree and the 2.6-meter tall bronze standing figure, but no surviving written records from the culture have been found to explain the purpose of these artifacts. To understand what these objects meant to the ancient Shu people, scholars have to connect scattered clues from other contemporary civilizations, draw analogies to later traditional Chinese sacrificial systems, and use imaginative reasoning to reconstruct the religious and social structure of ancient Shu. Without creativity, we would only have a pile of mysterious bronze objects, no accessible knowledge of the ancient Shu civilization at all.

Furthermore, creativity remains essential even when abundant written documents are available, because most historical documents are biased and incomplete, leaving large gaps in our understanding of the past. For centuries, mainstream historical narratives focused on the experiences of elites, kings and political leaders, because they were the ones who commissioned and preserved most official documents. The voices of ordinary people, such as workers, women and ethnic minorities, were rarely recorded in official archives. To reconstruct these forgotten histories, scholars have to exercise creativity to integrate scattered, unconventional sources of information. For example, when studying the daily lives of working-class women in 19th-century Britain, modern historians do not rely only on official factory reports which were written by male factory inspectors and largely biased against workers. Instead, they creatively combine fragmented sources including private diaries, parish birth and death records, oral history transcripts collected in the early 20th century, and even folk songs passed down among workers. By putting these clues together imaginatively, they have reconstructed a much more authentic picture of working women’s lives, which completely changed the previous one-sided narrative that painted all Victorian workers as homogeneous and uneducated. This achievement would have been impossible without creativity.

Admittedly, creativity in historical study is not unlimited imagination that can ignore evidence. Unlike novelists who can make up any plot to serve their story, historians must ground their creative interpretation in existing historical evidence, and any interpretation that contradicts solid archaeological or documentary evidence is not acceptable. Creativity in history is only a tool to fill the gaps left by incomplete evidence, not a license to fabricate fake history to serve a certain political or ideological purpose. When properly constrained, creativity actually helps us get closer to the objective truth of the past, rather than undermining objectivity.

In conclusion, the nature of historical study as a process of reconstructing an inaccessible past means that creativity is always required, even though history is generally considered an objective pursuit. The reason given by the claim correctly points out this core feature of historical learning. Properly used creativity, constrained by the available evidence, does not conflict with the pursuit of objectivity; instead, it enriches our understanding of the past and allows us to continuously update and correct our historical narratives to get closer to the full truth of what happened.
