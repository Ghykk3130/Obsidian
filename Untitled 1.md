The first article talks about the development of modern Chinese oil painting from the 1920s–1940s. The Chinese oil pointing in that period was influenced by Western styles via study in Japan and France. There were movements like the Storm Society and Chinese Independent Art Association, which embraced modernism (e.g., Fauvism, Surrealism). Key challenges included wartime destruction and political upheaval, leading to significant loss of artworks. The author also discusses the ideological debate between modernism and academic realism, exemplified by Xu Beihong. Ultimately, the war with Japan in 1937 shifted artistic focus from pure self-expression to social and political engagement, marking a watershed in Chinese modern art.

The second article is about how guohua societies in Republican Shanghai, particularly the Bee Society and the Painting Association of China, used modern institutional practices to legitimize and professionalize traditional Chinese art.  The author argues that this transformation was not only a reaction to Western influence but a strategic adaptation within a specific socio-cultural context. These societies shifted from commercial and social functions to promoting aesthetic ideologies through exhibitions, publications, and education. All of these contributed to elevating the status of guohua and reshaping artists' identities.
