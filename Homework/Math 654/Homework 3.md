# Acheson 7.2

We first compute the curl of $\mathbf{u} = u_\phi(r,\theta)\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$ in spherical coordinates. Since only the $\phi$-component is non-zero, the standard spherical curl formulas reduce to: $$\begin{align} & (\nabla \times \mathbf{u})_{r}= \frac{1}{r\sin \theta} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(u_{\phi}\sin \theta) \\ & (\nabla \times \mathbf{u})_{\theta}= - \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(ru_{\phi}) \\ & (\nabla \times \mathbf{u})_{\phi}=0 \end{align}$$ We then compute the double curl $[\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{u})]_\phi$ using the spherical identity $[\nabla\times\mathbf{F}]_\phi = \frac{1}{r}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(rF_\theta) - \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}F_r\right]$: $$\begin{align} [\nabla \times(\nabla \times \mathbf{u})]_{\phi} & = \frac{1}{r}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r(\nabla \times \mathbf{u})_{\theta})- \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(\nabla \times \mathbf{u})_{r} \right] \\ & = \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left( - \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(ru_{\phi}) \right)- \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left( \frac{1}{r\sin \theta} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(u_{\phi}\sin \theta) \right) \\ & = - \frac{1}{r}\left[ \frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial r^{2}}(ru_{\phi})+ \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left( \frac{1}{\sin \theta} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(u_{\phi}\sin \theta) \right) \right] \\ [\nabla \times(\nabla \times \mathbf{u})]_{r} & =0 \\ [\nabla \times(\nabla \times \mathbf{u})]_{\theta} & =0 \end{align}$$ The $r$ and $\theta$ components of $\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{u})$ are both zero because $(\nabla\times\mathbf{u})_\phi = 0$ and the formulas for those components involve only $\phi$-derivatives of $(\nabla\times\mathbf{u})_r$ and $(\nabla\times\mathbf{u})_\theta$ or cross-terms that vanish by symmetry.

The slow-flow (Stokes) equation is $\nabla p = -\mu\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{u})$. Since only the $\phi$-component of $\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{u})$ is non-zero, projecting onto each coordinate direction gives: $$\begin{align} & \nabla p= -\mu \nabla \times(\nabla \times \mathbf{u}) \\ \implies & \frac{\partial p}{\partial r} = 0, \quad \frac{\partial p}{\partial \theta} = 0, \\ \implies & \frac{1}{r\sin \theta} \frac{\partial p}{\partial \phi}= \frac{\mu}{r}\left[ \frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial r^{2}}(ru_{\phi})+ \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left( \frac{1}{\sin \theta} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(u_{\phi}\sin \theta) \right) \right] \end{align}$$ The right-hand side depends only on $r$ and $\theta$, so the general solution for $p$ takes the form $p = f(r,\theta)\phi + g(r,\theta)$ for arbitrary functions $f$ and $g$. However, the pressure field $p$ must be single-valued throughout the fluid domain. Since $\phi$ is a multi-valued coordinate (it is defined only modulo $2\pi$), the coefficient $f(r,\theta)$ must vanish identically. Hence $\frac{\partial p}{\partial \phi} = 0$, and the governing equation reduces to: $$\frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial r^{2}}(ru_{\phi})+ \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left[ \frac{1}{\sin \theta} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(u_{\phi}\sin \theta) \right]=0 \tag{7.72}$$ This shows that a purely rotary flow $\mathbf{u} = u_\phi(r,\theta)\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}$ is consistent with the slow-flow equations, provided $u_\phi$ satisfies the above.

For a point $(a,\theta,\phi)$ on the surface of the rotating sphere, the velocity is: $$\begin{align} \mathbf{v} & = \Omega \hat{\mathbf{z}}\times a \hat{\mathbf{r}} \\ & = a\Omega \sin \theta \hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \end{align}$$ using the standard identity $\hat{\mathbf{z}}\times\hat{\mathbf{r}} = \sin\theta,\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$. The fluid must match this velocity at $r=a$ by the no-slip condition, and must be quiescent at infinity: $$\begin{align} & \mathbf{u}=a\Omega \sin \theta \hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}\text{ at }r=a \\ & \mathbf{u}=0\text{ as }r\rightarrow \infty \end{align}$$ To solve equation (7.72), we separate the variables. Let $u_{\phi}=R(r)\Theta(\theta)$. Substituting into (7.72) and rearranging: $$\begin{align} & \Theta \frac{d^{2}}{dr^{2}}(rR)+ \frac{R}{r} \frac{d}{d\theta}\left( \frac{1}{\sin \theta} \frac{d}{d\theta}(\Theta \sin \theta) \right) =0 \\ \implies & \frac{r}{R} \frac{d^{2}}{dr^{2}}(rR)=- \frac{1}{\Theta} \frac{d}{d\theta}\left( \frac{1}{\sin \theta} \frac{d}{d\theta}(\Theta \sin \theta) \right) = \lambda \end{align}$$ where $\lambda$ is the separation constant. Expanding the left-hand side of the angular equation: $$\begin{align} & \frac{d}{d\theta}\left[ \frac{1}{\sin \theta}\left( \frac{d\Theta}{d\theta}\sin \theta+\Theta \cos \theta \right) \right]+\lambda \Theta=0 \\ \implies & \frac{d^{2}\Theta}{d\theta^{2}}+\cot \theta \frac{d\Theta}{d\theta}+\left( \lambda- \frac{1}{\sin^{2}\theta} \right)\Theta=0 \end{align}$$ Let $x=\cos \theta$. Then: $$(1-x^{2}) \frac{d^{2}\Theta}{dx^{2}}- 2x \frac{d\Theta}{dx}+\left( \lambda- \frac{1}{1-x^{2}} \right)\Theta=0$$This is precisely the associated Legendre equation with azimuthal order $m=1$. The solutions that are regular at $x=\pm 1$ (i.e., at $\theta = 0$ and $\theta=\pi$) are the associated Legendre functions $P_l^1(\cos\theta)$ for integers $l\geq 1$, with separation constant $\lambda = l(l+1)$.

With $\lambda = l(l+1)$, the radial equation becomes: $$\begin{align} & r \frac{d^{2}}{dr^{2}}(rR)=l(l+1)R \\ \implies & r^{2} R^{''}+2rR^{'}-l(l+1)R=0 \end{align}$$ This is an Euler–Cauchy ODE. We make the ansatz $R=r^{k}$, which yields the characteristic equation: $$\begin{align} & k(k-1)+2k-l(l+1)=0 \\ \implies & (k-l)(k+l+1)=0 \\ \implies & k=l\text{ or }-l-1 \end{align}$$ The general solution is therefore: $$u_{\phi}= \sum_{l=1}^{\infty}\left( a_{l}r^{l}+ \frac{b_{l}}{r^{l+1}} \right)P_{l}^{1}(\cos \theta)$$ The condition $u_\phi \to 0$ as $r\to\infty$ requires $a_l = 0$ for all $l$, since $r^l$ grows without bound. At $r = a$, we require $u_\phi(a,\theta) = a\Omega\sin\theta$. Since $P_1^1(\cos\theta) = \sin\theta$ and the associated Legendre polynomials form an orthogonal basis, only the $l=1$ term can contribute to match the $\sin\theta$ dependence of the boundary condition. Substituting in this result and fixing $b_1$: $$\begin{align} & \frac{b_{1}}{a^{2}}\sin \theta=a\Omega \sin \theta \\ \implies & b_{1}=\Omega a^{3} \end{align}$$ Then we conclude that: $$u_{\phi}(r,\theta)= \frac{\Omega a^{3}}{r^{2}}\sin \theta$$ We compute the stress tensor. The viscous stress component driving the azimuthal force on the sphere is $\sigma_{\phi r}$, which in spherical coordinates is: $$\begin{align} \sigma_{\phi,r} & = \mu\left( \frac{1}{r\sin \theta} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial \phi}+ r \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left( \frac{u_{\phi}}{r} \right) \right) \\ & = \mu r \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left( \frac{u_{\phi}}{r} \right) \\ & = \mu r\left( -3 \frac{\Omega a^{3}}{r^{4}}\sin \theta \right)=-3\mu \Omega\left( \frac{a^{3}}{r^{3}} \right)\sin \theta \\ \sigma_{\theta,r} & = \mu\left( r \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left( \frac{u_{\theta}}{r}\right)+ \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial \theta} \right)=0 \\ \sigma_{r,r} & = -p+ 2\mu \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial r}=-p \end{align}$$ Since the outward unit normal on the surface of the sphere is $\hat{\mathbf{r}}$, the surface traction vector has components: $$\begin{align} & T_{\phi} = \sigma_{\phi,r}\big|_{r=a}=- 3\mu \Omega \sin \theta \\ & T_{\theta}=\sigma_{\theta,r}\big|_{r=a}=0 \\ & T_{r}=\sigma_{r,r}\big|_{r=a}=-p \end{align}$$ Neither $T_{\theta}$ nor $T_{r}$ contributes to the torque about the $z$-axis: $T_r$ acts radially and $T_\theta$ acts in a meridional direction that is perpendicular to the rotation axis but without a moment arm in the azimuthal sense. The traction $T_{\phi}$ produces a torque with moment arm $a\sin\theta$ (the perpendicular distance from the $z$-axis). Integrating over the sphere surface: $$\begin{align} \tau^{'} & = \int_{0}^{2\pi}d\phi\int_{0}^{\pi}d\theta, a^{2}\sin \theta\cdot T_{\phi}\cdot(a\sin \theta) \\ & = -3\mu \Omega a^{3}(2\pi)\int_{0}^{\pi}d\theta\sin ^{3}\theta \end{align}$$ We evaluate the integral by writing $\sin^3\theta = \sin\theta(1-\cos^2\theta)$ and substituting $x=\cos\theta$: $$\begin{align} \int_{0}^{\pi}d\theta \sin ^{3}\theta & = \int d\theta \sin \theta (1-\cos ^{2}\theta) \\ & = -\int d(\cos \theta)(1-\cos ^{2}\theta) \\ & = -\left[ \cos \theta- \frac{\cos ^{3}\theta}{3} \right]_{0}^{\pi} \\ & = \frac{4}{3} \end{align}$$ Hence the torque exerted by the fluid on the sphere is $\tau' = -8\pi\mu\Omega a^3$. By Newton's third law, the torque that must be applied externally to maintain the rotation is equal and opposite: $$\tau = 8\pi\mu\Omega a^3$$
# Acheson 7.5

The two plates are located at $\theta = \pm\Omega t$ and move with angular velocities $\pm\Omega$. Applying the no-slip condition at each plate: $$\begin{align} & \text{At }\theta=+\Omega t : \quad u_{r}=0,\quad u_{\theta}=+\Omega r \\ & \text{At }\theta=-\Omega t : \quad u_{r}=0,\quad u_{\theta}=-\Omega r \end{align}$$ For a two-dimensional incompressible flow in polar coordinates, we introduce the stream function $\psi(r,\theta,t)$ defined by: $$u_{r}= \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial \theta},\qquad u_{\theta}= -\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial r}$$ Translating the boundary conditions into conditions on $\psi$: $$\begin{align} & -\left.\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial r}\right|_{\theta=\pm\Omega t}=\pm\Omega r \implies \psi(r,\pm\Omega t)=\mp\frac{1}{2}\Omega r^{2}+f(\theta) \\ & \left.\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial \theta}\right|_{\theta=\pm\Omega t}=0 \implies \left.\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial \theta}\right|_{\theta=\pm\Omega t}=0 \end{align}$$ The function $f$ arises as the constant of integration in $r$. Since $\psi$ must be regular at $r=0$ for all $\theta$, the function $f$ cannot depend on $\theta$ (otherwise the radial derivative $\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial r}|_{r=0}$ would be multi-valued). We therefore conclude $f$ is a pure constant, which we take to be zero without loss of generality. The boundary conditions on $\psi$ become: $$\begin{align} & \psi(r,+\Omega t)= -\frac{1}{2}\Omega r^{2} \\ & \psi(r,-\Omega t)= +\frac{1}{2}\Omega r^{2} \\ & \left.\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial \theta}\right|_{\theta=\pm\Omega t}=0 \end{align}$$ Under the slow-flow (low Reynolds number) assumption, the governing equations are $\nabla p = \mu\nabla^2\mathbf{u}$ and $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}=0$. Taking the curl of the momentum equation and using $\nabla\times(\nabla p)=\mathbf{0}$: $$0=\nabla \times \nabla p=\mu \nabla \times \nabla^{2}\mathbf{u}$$ We adopt Einstein summation convention to show $\nabla\times\nabla^2\mathbf{u} = \nabla^2(\nabla\times\mathbf{u})$: $$\begin{align} (\nabla \times \nabla^{2}\mathbf{u}) _{i} & = \epsilon_{ijk}\partial_{j}\partial_{l}^{2}u_{k} \\ & = \partial_{l}^{2}(\epsilon_{ijk}\partial_{j}u_{k}) \\ & = \nabla^{2}(\nabla \times \mathbf{u})_{i} \end{align}$$ Therefore $\nabla^{2}(\nabla \times \mathbf{u})=\mathbf{0}$. For this two-dimensional polar flow, the only non-trivial vorticity component is the out-of-plane one (introducing a $z$-axis perpendicular to the flow plane for convenience): $$\begin{align} (\nabla \times \mathbf{u})_{z} &= \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(ru_{\theta})- \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial \theta} \\ &= \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left( -r \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial r} \right)- \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left( \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial \theta} \right) \\ & = - \frac{\partial^{2}\psi}{\partial r^{2}}- \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial r}- \frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2}\psi}{\partial \theta^{2}} \\ & =-\nabla^{2}\psi \end{align}$$ Therefore $\nabla^{2}(\nabla \times \mathbf{u})_z = -\nabla^2(\nabla^2\psi) = 0$, which gives the biharmonic equation: $$\nabla^{4}\psi=0\implies\left( \frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial r^{2}} + \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}+ \frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial \theta^{2}} \right)^{2}\psi=0$$ We make the ansatz $\psi(r,\theta,t)= r^{2}g(\theta,t)$, motivated by the $r^2$ scaling of the boundary conditions. Computing $\nabla^{2}\psi$: $$\begin{align} \nabla^{2}\psi & = \frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial r^{2}}(r^{2}g)+ \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r^{2}g)+ \frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial \theta^{2}}(r^{2}g) \\ & = 2g+2g+ g'' = 4g+ g'' \end{align}$$ where primes denote $\partial/\partial\theta$. Since $4g+g''$ is a function of $\theta$ alone, applying $\nabla^2$ again: $$\begin{align} \nabla^{2}(\nabla^{2}\psi) & = \nabla^{2}\left( 4g+ g'' \right) \\ & = \frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial \theta^{2}}\left( 4g+ g'' \right) \\ & = \frac{1}{r^{2}}\left( 4 g''+ g^{(4)} \right)=0 \\ \implies & \quad g^{(4)}+ 4g'' =0 \end{align}$$ Integrating once: $$\begin{align} & g^{(3)}+4g' = A \\ \implies & g''+4g = A\theta+B \end{align}$$ We rewrite: $4(g+A\theta/4+B/4)+(g+A\theta/4+B/4)'' = 0$, so the general solution to the homogeneous part is $C\cos 2\theta + D\sin 2\theta$, and absorbing the particular solution into renamed constants, we obtain: $$g(\theta)= A\cos{2}\theta+B\sin 2\theta+C\theta+D$$ The four boundary conditions $g(\pm\Omega t) = \mp\frac{1}{2}\Omega$ and $g'(\pm\Omega t)=0$ give: $$\begin{align} & g(\Omega t)=- \frac{1}{2}\Omega \implies A\cos 2\Omega t + B\sin 2 \Omega t + C \Omega t + D = -\frac{1}{2}\Omega \\ & g(-\Omega t)= \frac{1}{2}\Omega \implies A\cos 2 \Omega t - B\sin 2\Omega t - C\Omega t + D = \frac{1}{2}\Omega \\ & g'(\Omega t)= 0 \implies -2A\sin 2\Omega t + 2B\cos 2\Omega t + C = 0 \\ & g'(-\Omega t)=0\implies 2A\sin 2\Omega t + 2B\cos 2\Omega t + C = 0 \end{align}$$ Adding and subtracting equations (3) and (4): $$\begin{align} & 2B\cos 2\Omega t +C=0 \\ & A \sin 2 \Omega t=0 \implies A=0 \end{align}$$ Adding and subtracting equations (1) and (2): $$\begin{align} & 2 A \cos 2\Omega t+2D=0\implies D=0 \\ & 2B \sin 2\Omega t+ 2C \Omega t =-\Omega \end{align}$$ Substituting $C = -2B\cos 2\Omega t$ into the last equation: $$\begin{align} & 2B \sin 2\Omega t+ 2(-2B\cos 2\Omega t)\Omega t=-\Omega \\ \implies & B= \frac{-\Omega /2}{\sin 2\Omega t-2\Omega t \cos 2\Omega t} \end{align}$$ and therefore: $$C = -2B\cos 2\Omega t = \frac{\Omega \cos 2 \Omega t}{\sin 2\Omega t-2 \Omega t \cos 2\Omega t}$$ Substituting back: $$\begin{align} g(\theta) & = \frac{-\Omega /2}{\sin 2\Omega t-2\Omega t \cos 2\Omega t} \sin 2\theta+ \frac{\Omega \cos 2\Omega t}{\sin 2 \Omega t-2\Omega t \cos 2\Omega t}\theta \\ & = \frac{- \Omega /2}{\sin 2\Omega t-2\Omega t\cos 2\Omega t}(\sin 2\theta-2\theta \cos 2\Omega t) \end{align}$$ The stream function is therefore: $$\psi = - \frac{1}{2}\Omega r^{2}\left( \frac{\sin 2\theta-2\theta \cos 2\Omega t}{\sin 2\Omega t-2\Omega t \cos 2\Omega t} \right)$$ in agreement with equation (7.74).

If $2\Omega t = \pi/2$, we have $\sin 2\Omega t = 1$ and $\cos 2\Omega t = 0$, so: $$\begin{align} \psi & = - \frac{1}{2}\Omega r^{2}\cdot\frac{\sin{2}\theta}{1} \\ & = -\Omega r^{2}\sin \theta \cos \theta \\ & = -\Omega xy \end{align}$$ The streamlines $\psi = \text{const.}$ are therefore the curves $xy = \text{const.}$, which are rectangular hyperbolas with asymptotes along the coordinate axes — consistent with the geometry of the opening wedge at right angles.

From the stream function at $2\Omega t = \pi/2$: $$\begin{align} u_{r} & = \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial \theta} = -\Omega r \cos 2\theta \sim \mathcal{O}(\Omega r) \\ u_{\theta} & = - \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial r} = -\Omega r \sin 2\theta\sim \mathcal{O}(\Omega r) \end{align}$$ The characteristic velocity scale is thus $U\sim \mathcal{O}(\Omega r)$. Since the plate positions evolve on the timescale $T\sim \mathcal{O}(1/\Omega)$, we estimate the magnitudes of the three terms in the full Navier–Stokes equation: $$\begin{align} & \left| \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} \right|\sim \mathcal{O}\left( \frac{U}{T} \right) \sim \mathcal{O}(\Omega^{2}r) \\ & |\mathbf{u}\cdot \nabla \mathbf{u} |\sim \mathcal{O}\left( U \cdot \frac{U}{r} \right) \sim \mathcal{O}(\Omega^{2}r) \\ & |\nu \nabla^{2}\mathbf{u}|\sim \mathcal{O}\left( \nu \frac{U}{r^{2}} \right)\sim \mathcal{O}\left( \frac{\nu \Omega}{r} \right) \end{align}$$ For the slow-flow approximation to be valid, the viscous term must dominate over both the inertial and unsteady terms. This requires: $$\Omega^{2}r\ll \frac{\nu \Omega}{r}\implies \frac{\Omega r^{2}}{\nu}\ll 1$$ This is the local Reynolds number condition. No matter how large the viscosity $\nu$, for sufficiently large $r$ the condition will eventually be violated and the slow-flow approximation breaks down.
# Acheson 8.2

Since the flow is two-dimensional and incompressible ($\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}=0$), we may introduce a stream function $\psi$ such that $u = \frac{\partial\psi}{\partial y}$ and $v = -\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial x}$. We seek a similarity solution of the form $u(x,y) = \alpha x f'(\eta)$, where $\eta = y/g(x)$. Integrating the first relation with respect to $y$ from the stagnation point (where $\psi = 0$ by convention, since only its derivatives are physical, we are free to choose its zero point): $$\begin{align} & \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial y}=u \\ \implies & \psi=\int_{\text{stagnation point}}^{y} dy',u =\int d(\eta' g(x)), \alpha x f'(\eta')= \alpha x g(x)f(\eta) \end{align}$$ This expression confirms the ansatz $\psi = \alpha x g(x) f(\eta)$, with $f(0) = 0$ enforcing the no-penetration condition at the wall. The surface of the body naturally defines a streamline since no flow can penetrate it, so $\psi = 0$ on the surface without loss of generality.

Differentiating $\psi$ with respect to $x$ (noting that $\eta = y/g(x)$ also depends on $x$ via $\frac{\partial\eta}{\partial x} = -yg'/g^2 = -\eta g'/g$): $$\begin{align} v & = - \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x} \\ & = -\alpha g(x)f(\eta)- \frac{\partial(\alpha x g(x) f(\eta))}{\partial \eta} \frac{\partial \eta}{\partial x} \\ & = -\alpha g(x)f(\eta)- \alpha x g(x) f'(\eta)\left( - \frac{\eta g^{'}}{g} \right) \\ & = -\alpha f (g+x g^{'})+ \alpha x \eta g^{'}f^{'} \end{align}$$ Here the $x$ derivative in the first step extracts all $x$ dependence including through $\eta$, while the chain rule in the second step separates the explicit $x$ dependence from the implicit dependence through $\eta$. The necessary spatial derivatives of $u = \alpha x f'(\eta)$ are: $$\begin{align} & \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(\alpha x f^{'})= \alpha f^{'}+\alpha xf^{''}\left( - \eta \frac{g^{'}}{g} \right) \\ & \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}=\alpha x f^{''} \frac{1}{g} \\ & \frac{\partial^{2}u}{\partial y^{2}}= \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left( \frac{\alpha x}{g} f^{''} \right)= \frac{\alpha x}{g^{2}}f^{'''} \end{align}$$ The convective terms are: $$\begin{align} & u \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}= \alpha x f^{'}\left( \alpha f^{'}- \alpha x \eta \frac{g^{'}}{g}f^{''} \right)= \alpha^{2}x f^{'2}-\alpha^{2} x^{2} \eta \frac{g^{'}}{g} f^{'}f^{''} \\ & v \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}=(-\alpha f(g+xg^{'})+\alpha x\eta g^{'}f^{'})\left( \frac{\alpha x }{g} f^{''} \right) = -\alpha^{2} x\left( 1+ \frac{xg^{'}}{g} \right)ff^{''}+ \alpha^{2} x^{2} \eta \frac{g^{'}}{g} f^{'}f^{''} \end{align}$$ Adding these, the cross terms involving $\eta$ cancel exactly, leaving: $$u\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+v\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = \alpha^{2}x\left[f'^2 - \left(1+\frac{xg'}{g}\right)ff''\right]$$ The boundary-layer momentum equation is $u\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+v\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = U\frac{dU}{dx}+\nu\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}$, with $U(x)=\alpha x$ so $U\frac{dU}{dx}=\alpha^2 x$. Substituting: $$\begin{align} & \alpha^{2}x f^{'2}-\alpha^{2}x\left( 1+ \frac{xg^{'}}{g} \right)f f^{''}=\alpha^{2}x + \frac{\nu \alpha x}{g^{2}}f^{'''} \\ \implies & \frac{\nu}{\alpha g^{2}(x)}f^{'''}(\eta)+\left( 1+ \frac{xg^{'}(x)}{g(x)} \right)f(\eta) f^{''}(\eta)+1-f^{'2}(\eta)=0 \end{align}$$ Since $x$ and $\eta$ are independent variables, for this to be an ODE in $\eta$ alone the coefficients of $f'''$ and $ff''$ must each be independent of $x$. If $g(x)$ is not constant, then $f(\eta)$ would need to satisfy a different differential equation at each value of $x$, which is impossible for any non-trivial $f$. Therefore we must conclude $g(x) = \text{const.}$, which sets $g' = 0$ and both coefficients become constants. The ODE reduces to:
$$\frac{\nu}{\alpha g^{2}}f^{'''}(\eta)+ff^{''}+1-f^{'2}=0 $$ We rescale: $\tilde{\eta}=\lambda \eta$, $\tilde{f}(\tilde{\eta})=\gamma f(\eta)$. Then: $$\frac{d}{d \eta}f= \frac{\lambda}{\gamma} \frac{d}{d \tilde{\eta}} \tilde{f}$$ Know that: $$u=\alpha x \frac{d}{d \eta}f= \frac{\lambda}{\gamma}\alpha x \frac{d}{d \tilde{\eta}}\tilde{f}$$ We choose $\gamma=\lambda$ so that $u = \alpha x f'(\eta) = \alpha x \tilde{f}'(\tilde{\eta})$ is preserved. Then: $$\begin{align} & \frac{d^{2}}{d \eta^{2}}f= \lambda \frac{d^{2}}{d \tilde{\eta}^{2}}\tilde{f} \\ & \frac{d^{3}}{d \eta^{3}} f= \lambda^{2} \frac{d^{3}}{d \tilde{\eta}^{3}}\tilde{f} \end{align}$$ Substituting into $(_)$: $$\begin{align} & \frac{\nu}{\alpha g^{2}} \lambda^{2} \frac{d^{3}}{d \tilde{\eta}^{3}}\tilde{f}+ \frac{1}{\lambda}\tilde{f} \cdot \lambda \frac{d^{2}}{d \tilde{\eta}^{2}} \tilde{f}+1- \left( \frac{d}{d \tilde{\eta}}\tilde{f} \right) ^{2}=0 \\ \implies & \left( \frac{\nu \lambda^{2}}{\alpha g^{2}} \right) \frac{d^{3}}{d \tilde{\eta}^{3}}\tilde{f}+ \tilde{f} \frac{d^{2}}{d \tilde{\eta}^{2}}\tilde{f}+1- \left( \frac{d}{d \tilde{\eta}}\tilde{f} \right)^{2}=0 \end{align}$$ Setting $\frac{\nu \lambda^{2}}{\alpha g^{2}}=1$ gives $\lambda=g \sqrt{ \frac{\alpha}{\nu} }$, so $\tilde{\eta}=\lambda \eta= g \frac{\sqrt{ \alpha }}{\sqrt{ \nu }}\cdot \frac{y}{g}= \frac{y}{\sqrt{ \nu /\alpha }}$. Dropping tildes for notational clarity, the problem reduces to: $$f^{'''}+ f f^{''}+1- f^{'2}=0$$ At $y=0$ (the wall): no-slip requires $u = \alpha x f'(0) = 0$, so $f'(0)=0$; no-penetration requires $v = 0$. Computing $v$ at $y=0$ with $g = (\nu/\alpha)^{1/2}$: $$v(x,0)= - \alpha \sqrt{ \frac{\nu}{\alpha} },f(0)=0\implies f(0)=0$$ As $y\to\infty$, matching to the mainstream $u\to U(x) = \alpha x$ requires $f'(\infty) = 1$. In summary: $$f(0) = f'(0) = 0, \qquad f'(\infty) = 1$$
# Acheson 8.3

The stream function is $\psi = F(x)f(\eta)$ with $\eta = y/g(x)$. Differentiating with respect to $y$: $$\begin{align} u & = \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial y}= \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(F(x)f(\eta)) \\ & = F(x)f^{'}(\eta) \frac{\partial \eta}{\partial y}= \frac{F(x)}{g(x)}f^{'}(\eta) \end{align}$$ Imposing the mainstream condition as $\eta \rightarrow \infty$: $$\begin{align} \lim_{ \eta \rightarrow \infty } u & = U(x) \\ \implies \frac{F(x)}{g(x)}\lim_{ \eta \rightarrow \infty } f^{'}(\eta) & = U(x) \end{align}$$ Since $\lim_{ \eta \rightarrow \infty } f^{'}(\eta)$ must be a constant, say $c$, if the limit exists, we have: $$c \frac{F(x)}{g(x)} = U(x)\implies F(x)= \frac{1}{c}U(x)g(x)$$ For convenience, choose $c=1$. We then have $F(x)=U(x)g(x)$. The stream function and the $x$-component velocity are: $$\begin{align} \psi & = U(x)g(x)f(\eta) \\ u & = U(x)f^{'}(\eta) \end{align}$$ We compute the necessary derivatives for the momentum equation. First, the $y$-component velocity, differentiating $\psi$ with respect to $x$ and using $\frac{\partial\eta}{\partial x} = -\eta g'/g$: $$\begin{align} v & = - \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x}= - \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(Ugf) \\ & = -(U^{'}g+Ug^{'})f-Ugf^{'} \frac{\partial \eta}{\partial x} \\ & = -(U^{'}g+Ug^{'})f-Ugf^{'}\left( -\eta \frac{g^{'}}{g} \right) \\ & = -(U^{'}g+Ug^{'})f+U\eta g^{'}f^{'} \end{align}$$ Then the spatial derivatives of $u$: $$\begin{align} \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} & = U^{'}f^{'}+Uf^{''}\left( -\eta \frac{g^{'}}{g} \right)= U^{'}f^{'}-U\eta \frac{g^{'}}{g}f^{''} \\ \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} & = Uf^{''} \frac{1}{g}= \frac{U}{g}f^{''} \\ \frac{\partial^{2}u}{\partial y^{2}} & = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left( \frac{U}{g}f^{''} \right)= \frac{U}{g^{2}}f^{'''} \end{align}$$ We construct the convective terms: $$\begin{align} u \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} & = UU^{'}f^{'2}-U^{2}\eta \frac{g^{'}}{g}f^{'}f^{''} \\ v \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} & = \left( -(U^{'}g+Ug^{'})f+U\eta g^{'}f^{'} \right)\left( \frac{U}{g}f^{''} \right) \\ & = -U\left( U^{'}+U \frac{g^{'}}{g} \right)ff^{''}+U^{2}\eta \frac{g^{'}}{g}f^{'}f^{''} \end{align}$$ Adding them up, the cross terms containing $\eta$ exactly cancel out: $$\begin{align} u \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+v \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} & = UU^{'}f^{'2}-U\left( U^{'}+U \frac{g^{'}}{g} \right)ff^{''} \\ & = UU^{'} \left[ f^{'2}-\left( 1+ \frac{Ug^{'}}{U^{'}g} \right)ff^{''} \right] \end{align}$$ Substituting into the boundary-layer momentum equation $u \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+ v \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = U \frac{dU}{dx}+ \nu \frac{\partial^{2}u}{\partial y^{2}}$: $$UU^{'} \left[ f^{'2}-\left( 1+ \frac{Ug^{'}}{U^{'}g} \right)ff^{''} \right] = UU^{'}+\nu \frac{U}{g^{2}}f^{'''}$$ Assuming $U^{'} \neq 0$, dividing both sides by $UU^{'}$ (if $U^{'}=0$, it is just the Blasius flow treated in the last problem, which is not our interest here): $$f^{'2}-\left( 1+ \frac{Ug^{'}}{U^{'}g} \right)ff^{''}= 1+ \frac{\nu}{g^{2}U^{'}}f^{'''}$$ For this equation to admit a similarity solution $f(\eta)$, the coefficients must be independent of $x$, as we argued in Acheson 8.2. We introduce constants $C_{1}$ and $C_{2}$: $$\begin{align} \frac{\nu}{g^{2}U^{'}} & = C_{1} \\ 1+ \frac{Ug^{'}}{U^{'}g} & = C_{2} \end{align}$$ From the first condition, we have $g^{2}= \frac{\nu}{C_{1}U^{'}}$. Differentiating with respect to $x$: $$\begin{align} 2gg^{'} & = - \frac{\nu U^{''}}{C_{1}U^{'2}} \\ \implies \frac{g^{'}}{g} & = \frac{1}{2g^{2}}\left( - \frac{\nu U^{''}}{C_{1}U^{'2}} \right)= \frac{C_{1}U^{'}}{2\nu}\left( - \frac{\nu U^{''}}{C_{1}U^{'2}} \right)= - \frac{U^{''}}{2U^{'}} \end{align}$$ Substituting this into the second condition: $$\begin{align} 1+ \frac{U}{U^{'}}\left( - \frac{U^{''}}{2U^{'}} \right) & = C_{2} \\ \implies \frac{UU^{''}}{U^{'2}} & = 2(1-C_{2}) \equiv K \end{align}$$ We solve the differential equation $UU^{''}=KU^{'2}$, which can be rewritten as: $$\frac{U^{''}}{U^{'}} = K \frac{U^{'}}{U}$$ Integrating with respect to $x$: $$\begin{align} \int \frac{dU^{'}}{U^{'}} & = K\int \frac{dU}{U} \\ \ln U^{'} & = K\ln U + \ln C \\ U^{'} & = CU^{K} \end{align}$$ This is a separable equation $\frac{dU}{U^{K}}=C,dx$. We consider two cases based on the value of $K$.

If $K=1$: $$\begin{align} \int \frac{dU}{U} & = \int C,dx \\ \ln U & = Cx+D \\ \implies U(x) & \propto e^{\alpha x} \end{align}$$ If $K \neq 1$: $$\begin{align} \int U^{-K}dU & = \int C,dx \\ \frac{U^{1-K}}{1-K} & = Cx+D \\ U^{1-K} & = (1-K)(Cx+D) \end{align}$$ Setting $m= \frac{1}{1-K}$, we obtain: $$U(x) \propto (x-x_{0})^{m}$$ Consider the specific case $U(x)=Ax^{m}$ with $A>0$. Then $U^{'}=mAx^{m-1}$. From the condition $g^{2}U^{'}=\text{const.}$, we have: $$\begin{align} g^{2}\cdot mAx^{m-1} & \propto 1 \\ \implies g(x) & \propto x^{ \frac{1-m}{2} } \end{align}$$ Choose the specific proportional constant as given in the problem: $$g(x) = \left[ \frac{2\nu}{(m+1)Ax^{m-1}} \right]^{ \frac{1}{2} }$$ We evaluate the two constant coefficients in the differential equation with this choice of $g(x)$. First coefficient: $$\begin{align} g^{2}U^{'} & = \frac{2\nu}{(m+1)Ax^{m-1}}\cdot mAx^{m-1} = \frac{2m\nu}{m+1} \\ \implies \frac{\nu}{g^{2}U^{'}} & = \frac{m+1}{2m} \end{align}$$ Second coefficient: since $g \propto x^{ \frac{1-m}{2} }$, we have $\frac{g^{'}}{g}= \frac{1-m}{2x}$. Also $\frac{U}{U^{'}}= \frac{x}{m}$. Then: $$1+ \frac{U}{U^{'}} \frac{g^{'}}{g} = 1+\left( \frac{x}{m} \right)\left( \frac{1-m}{2x} \right)= 1+ \frac{1-m}{2m}= \frac{m+1}{2m}$$ Both coefficients are equal to $\frac{m+1}{2m}$, which serves as a non-trivial self-consistency check. Substituting back into the differential equation: $$f^{'2}-\left( \frac{m+1}{2m} \right)ff^{''} = 1+\left( \frac{m+1}{2m} \right)f^{'''}$$ Multiplying by $\frac{2m}{m+1}$: $$\begin{align} \frac{2m}{m+1}f^{'2}-ff^{''} & = \frac{2m}{m+1}+f^{'''} \\ \implies f^{'''}+ff^{''}+ \frac{2m}{m+1}(1-f^{'2}) & = 0 \end{align}$$ The boundary conditions are: $$\begin{align} u(x,0) & = U(x)f^{'}(0)=0\implies f^{'}(0)=0 \\ v(x,0) & = 0\implies f(0)=0 \\ u(x,\infty) & = U(x)f^{'}(\infty)=U(x)\implies f^{'}(\infty)=1 \end{align}$$ These are the Falkner–Skan equations.

For flow past a flat plate, the mainstream velocity is constant $U(x)=U_{0}$, corresponding to $m=0$. It is worth noting an apparent mathematical contradiction in the derivation: during the intermediate steps, we explicitly assumed $U^{'} \neq 0$ to divide the momentum equation by $UU^{'}$, which seemingly precludes the constant mainstream velocity case where $U^{'} = 0$. Consequently, the coefficient $\frac{m+1}{2m}$ exhibits an algebraic singularity at $m=0$. However, this contradiction is resolved in the final step when we multiply the entire equation by $\frac{2m}{m+1}$. This operation acts as an algebraic restoration that effectively eliminates the removable singularity in the parameter space. By taking the limit as $m \rightarrow 0$, the term $\frac{2m}{m+1}(1-f^{'2})$ smoothly vanishes, and the Falkner–Skan equation degenerates into the classic Blasius equation: $$f^{'''}+ff^{''}=0$$ For flow near a forward stagnation point, $U(x)=\alpha x$, corresponding to $m=1$. Substituting: $$f^{'''}+ff^{''}+ \frac{2}{2}(1-f^{'2}) = 0\implies f^{'''}+ff^{''}+1-f^{'2}=0$$ which agrees precisely with the result derived independently in Acheson 8.2, providing a satisfying consistency check.
