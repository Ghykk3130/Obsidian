The first reading examines the profound impact of the Ming dynasty’s collapse in 1644 on Chinese literati artists. The author frames the event as a collective trauma, forcing artists to navigate dual identities as “remnant subjects” (yimin) of the fallen Ming or servitors to the new Qing. Through case studies like Xiang Shengmo and Gong Xian, the author explores how artists expressed loyalty, grief, and defiance—often indirectly—using metaphors like the “wilderness” and motifs of exile. 

The second text examines the development of Ming literati painting theory, focusing on the influential Northern and Southern Schools classification formulated by Tung Ch’i-ch’ang and his circle. This theory was not purely art historical but also a manifesto that linked artistic lineage (from Wang Wei and Li Ssu-hsün) to Ch’an Buddhist concepts of enlightenment, valuing the Southern School’s “sudden” inspiration and amateur ideal. The author traces the theory's antecedents and highlights its dual criteria—stylistic tradition and the artist's social status—effectively creating an orthodox lineage for scholar-painters while marginalizing professional academies.
