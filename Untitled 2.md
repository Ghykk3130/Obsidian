The literati painting theory of the Northern Song period, primarily articulated by Su Shi and Huang Tingjian, established a foundational distinction between the "scholar's painting" and that of professional artisans. They posited that true artistic value lay not in formal likeness or technical skill, but in the expression of the artist's innate character, scholarly cultivation, and poetic sensibility. This philosophy emphasized spontaneous, effortless creation aligned with the "Tao," arguing that a superior man, through his erudition and moral integrity, could naturally master the brush. Consequently, the artwork was valued as a conduit of the artist's spirit, with its lingering resonance and suggested meaning—its "overtone"—being more significant than representational accuracy.

Empress Yang Meizi’s art reveals a sophisticated use of the yongwu (celebrating objects) tradition as a vehicle for political and personal expression. Through poetic inscriptions on paintings of blossoms and water, she crafted a nuanced self-image, shifting from themes of youthful beauty and fertility to mature virtue and Daoist-inflected authority. These works, often collaborations with court painters like Ma Yuan, functioned as intimate communications with Emperor Ningzong and strategic gifts to political allies. Her Water Album, in particular, uses water’s fluid power as a metaphor for discreet female authority, advising her clan on survival through adaptability and yielding strength.
